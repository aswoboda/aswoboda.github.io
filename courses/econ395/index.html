Cities are extraordinary social experiments. This course teaches you to analyze them.

The Course

Where you live shapes nearly everything about your economic life — your school, your commute, your exposure to pollution, your access to opportunity. Urban economics takes that seriously. This course gives you the tools to study it rigorously.

Cities are constantly generating policy changes, infrastructure investments, and demographic shifts — each one a potential natural experiment. We study them with the methods of applied microeconomics: difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, and hedonic pricing. The goal is not to survey urban economics broadly, but to develop genuine facility with causal inference and apply it to questions that matter.

Questions We Study

The city as a laboratory

Urban economists study questions where the answer matters for real people in specific places — and where careful research design can get us close to a causal answer. Here is a sample of the kinds of questions this seminar takes seriously.

Housing & Affordability
St. Paul, MN · San Francisco, CA

When cities enact rent control, does it protect tenants — or shrink the housing supply they depend on? What does the evidence actually show?

Neighborhood Change
Cities across the U.S.

When a neighborhood gentrifies, who benefits and who is displaced? Can we measure the causal effect of rising rents on longtime residents?

Transportation
Twin Cities · Seattle, WA

A new transit station opens and nearby property values rise. But whose? Does better transit access reduce inequality, or mainly raise the cost of living close to it?

Environmental Quality
Polluting facilities, U.S.

Who ends up living near highways, industrial sites, and landfills? Is it income, race, or something else driving the pattern — and how would we know?

Schools & Opportunity
School district boundaries

Parents pay large premiums to live near high-rated schools. How do we separate the value of school quality from the value of the neighborhood — and does school quality actually change outcomes?

Cities & the Economy
220 U.S. metro areas

Zoning restrictions in New York and San Francisco prevent workers from moving to high-productivity places. How much does that cost the national economy?

These are illustrative questions, not a fixed syllabus. Part of this seminar is learning to formulate your own.

What You'll Do

Three things, done well

01

Read like a researcher

We read empirical papers actively — diagnosing identification strategies, locating assumptions, asking what a better design would look like. You will leave able to evaluate applied economic research, not just describe it.

02

Replicate to understand

Before building your own study, you will work through an existing paper's data and methods. Understanding why a research design works — or doesn't — is the foundation for designing something better.

03

Design a study

The central deliverable is a well-specified research prospectus: a question, a natural experiment, a comparison group, the data you would need, and an honest accounting of what could go wrong.

Example Papers

Six threads, six landmarks

Urban economics is organized around durable research threads — each one built up over decades, each one still producing new work. Below are six papers worth reading before the term begins: one landmark per thread, chosen because each one asks a clean question, exploits a natural experiment, and produced a result that surprised people. That combination is the template.

A note on theory. Before we engage the empirical literature, we start with the Alonso-Muth-Mills monocentric city model — a simple, elegant theory of how land use and rents should vary with distance from the city center. Its predictions are testable with basic data. One of the first things we do is ask: how well does it hold up? That question turns out to open most of the threads below.
Housing & Rent Control
The Effects of Rent Control Expansion on Tenants, Landlords, and Inequality
Diamond, McQuade & Qian · AER 2019

A 1994 SF ballot initiative accidentally created a natural experiment. Rent control helped incumbents stay — but landlords converted to condos, ultimately raising citywide rents and accelerating gentrification. The policy achieved the opposite of its goal.
Schools & Sorting
Do Better Schools Matter? Parental Valuation of Elementary Education
Black · QJE 1999

A boundary discontinuity design: compare houses on opposite sides of an attendance district line. They're in the same neighborhood, pay the same taxes — they differ only in which elementary school the child attends. The premium for the better school is large and precisely estimated.
Cities & Macro Growth
Housing Constraints and Spatial Misallocation
Hsieh & Moretti · AEJ: Macro 2019

Zoning in New York and San Francisco prevents workers from moving to high-productivity cities. The aggregate cost: U.S. GDP growth was roughly 36% lower from 1964 to 2009 than it would have been with freer housing markets. A local policy question with national stakes.
Neighborhoods & Mobility
The Opportunity Atlas
Chetty, Friedman, Hendren et al. · NBER 2018

Using tax records covering nearly all Americans, this project maps intergenerational mobility at the neighborhood level. Where you grow up matters enormously for where you end up — and the variation is hyper-local. Click the link and look up where you're from.
Environment & Health
Does Air Quality Matter? Evidence from the Housing Market
Chay & Greenstone · JPE 2005

The Clean Air Act forced some counties to reduce pollution and left others unchanged. A natural experiment. Counties with cleaner air saw property values rise. The hedonic framework reveals what people actually pay for clean air — and the answer is large.
Transportation
Effect of Light Rail Transit on Nearby Property Values: Evidence from Seattle
Dubé et al. · JTLU 2018

A clean DiD design around Seattle's new light rail. The answer wasn't what transit advocates expected: effects varied dramatically by station and were negative in some neighborhoods. Context, not just infrastructure, determines outcomes — a lesson that runs through the whole course.

Links go to freely accessible versions. Carleton library provides access to paywalled journals for all assigned readings.

Where the Research Lives

The journals of urban economics

Want to see what researchers in this field are working on right now? The journals below publish the kind of empirical work this seminar engages with. Browsing a recent table of contents — even just the titles — is a fast way to get a sense of how broad the field actually is.

JUE
Journal of Urban Economics

The flagship journal of the field, founded in 1974 by Edwin Mills — one of the architects of the monocentric city model. Publishes empirical and theoretical work across housing, labor markets, transportation, crime, and local public finance.
RSUE
Regional Science and Urban Economics

Broader geographic scope than JUE, covering both urban and regional questions. Strong on spatial methods, agglomeration economies, and land use. Many hedonic pricing papers appear here.
AEJ:POL
American Economic Journal: Economic Policy

Not exclusively urban, but publishes some of the most-read applied work on housing, zoning, environmental regulation, and place-based policy. Papers here tend to have the cleanest identification strategies and the widest readership outside the field.
JTLU
Journal of Transport and Land Use

Open access. Focused on the intersection of transportation systems and land use — transit, accessibility, housing near stations, and induced demand. One of the few major urban journals where everything is freely readable without institutional access.

Research in 2026

The modern research toolkit

The tools available for doing economic research are changing fast. We will experiment with a range of tools across the research process, using a live research question as our shared testbed. The goal is not to prescribe a workflow but to build enough hands-on experience that you can form your own informed view of what helps and what doesn't. Strong opinions about these tools are common. This seminar is an opportunity to build your own grounded view.

Finding & Synthesizing Literature
Elicit

An AI-powered research assistant built for academic literature. Useful for generating a first map of a research area — who has studied this question, with what methods, and what did they find? Worth learning to query well; outputs improve significantly with practice.

Connected Papers

Visualizes the citation network around a seed paper — which papers are central to a literature, which are recent departures, what the field considers foundational. A useful complement to keyword search for understanding how a research area is structured.

NotebookLM

Upload a set of PDFs and interrogate them as a collection. Particularly useful early in a research project: "What identification strategies do these papers use?" "Where do they disagree about mechanisms?" Produces a contained knowledge base rather than a freeform web search — which turns out to matter.

Working with Data & Code
Claude Code

AI-assisted coding in R and Python. We use this in the replication exercise — not to generate code blindly, but to understand what the code does and why. The skill being developed is working with AI-generated code critically: debugging, interpreting output, catching mistakes.

Claude

Useful across the research process: interrogating a paper ("what is the identification assumption and is it plausible here?"), drafting and refining written work, thinking through research design. Works best when you bring genuine questions and push back on answers that seem too easy.

Comps Connection

This seminar is your launchpad

The research prospectus you develop here is the formal gateway to senior Comps. Two paths are available — and this seminar prepares you for both.

This seminar takes presenting and defending research seriously — not as a performance requirement, but as a skill that develops with practice. You will have multiple opportunities to explain research designs out loud across the term, starting early and at low stakes. Early presentations focus on formative feedback and format familiarity, serving as practice before later presentations that expect more polish and depth of expertise in your area of study. By the time you are presenting work you have spent weeks developing, the format itself should feel familiar rather than daunting.

The prospectus is due at the end of Week 7. By Week 9 you will know whether it has been approved, and — if approved — you may choose either Comps path. If you are undecided between research and policy careers, this seminar does not force the choice prematurely.

Primary Research Paper

Modeled on academic research. A full empirical study — question, literature, data, methods, results — typically 25–40 pages. The natural path for students considering graduate school or research-oriented careers.

Policy White Paper

Modeled on think tank and government reports. A rigorous, evidence-based argument for a specific policy position — typically 15–20 pages. The natural path for students headed toward policy, advocacy, or public service.

White Paper Topics · Fall 2026

Available topics for the Policy White Paper path

Students choosing the Policy White Paper path select one of the following topics. Each is chosen because the economics is genuinely interesting, the stakes are real, and the evidence base is substantial enough to support a rigorous argument.

Housing & Opportunity
Housing Mobility and Opportunity

Should the federal government expand housing mobility programs to expand opportunity for low-income families?

Where you grow up shapes your life outcomes in ways that are now well-documented by economists. Children who grow up in high-opportunity neighborhoods earn more, are less likely to be incarcerated, and are more likely to attend college than observationally similar children who grow up in low-opportunity neighborhoods — and these effects are causal, not just correlational. Yet access to high-opportunity neighborhoods is increasingly limited by housing costs, discrimination, and the design of housing assistance programs.

A strong paper will engage seriously with the evidence on neighborhood effects and the Moving to Opportunity experiment, the implementation challenges that have limited the effectiveness of existing voucher programs (landlord discrimination, search costs, information gaps), the tension between mobility-based and place-based approaches to addressing concentrated poverty, and the political economy of housing policy at the federal and local level. The goal is a policy argument that takes the empirical evidence seriously while being honest about what it does and does not establish.

Key Literature
  • Chetty, R., Hendren, N., & Katz, L. (2016). The effects of exposure to better neighborhoods on children. American Economic Review, 106(4), 855–902.
  • Chetty, R., & Hendren, N. (2018). The impacts of neighborhoods on intergenerational mobility. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(3), 1107–1162.
  • Chetty, R., Friedman, J., Hendren, N., Jones, M., & Porter, S. (2018). The Opportunity Atlas. NBER Working Paper 25147.
  • Chetty, R., et al. (2022). Social capital and economic mobility. Nature, 608.

Local Governance & Technology
Data Centers and Local Communities

What should cities ask for when a data center comes to town?

Data centers are arriving in communities across the United States at an unprecedented pace, bringing promises of tax revenue and jobs alongside concerns about noise, water use, electricity costs, and land use. Cities and counties are being asked to make rapid decisions — approving zoning variances, negotiating tax abatements, establishing community benefit agreements, and setting utility rate structures — often with limited information and under significant pressure from developers and state economic development officials.

A strong paper will engage seriously with the economics of bargaining between local governments and mobile capital, the fiscal incidence of tax incentives (who actually bears the cost of a property tax abatement?), the externalities that communities have legitimate claims to mitigate, and the political economy of why cities often end up with less than they could have negotiated. You should draw on real examples from the seminar's site portfolio and produce a policy argument that a city council member or economic development director could actually use.

Key Literature
  • Slattery, C. & Zidar, O. (2020). Evaluating state and local business tax incentives. Journal of Economic Perspectives, 34(2), 90–118.
  • Greenstone, M. & Moretti, E. (2003). Bidding for industrial plants. NBER Working Paper 9844.
  • Gross, J. (2005). Community benefit agreements: Making development projects accountable. Partnership for Working Families.
  • Zodrow, G. & Mieszkowski, P. (1986). Pigou, Tiebout, property taxation, and the underprovision of local public goods. Journal of Urban Economics, 19(3), 356–370.
