The Course

Microeconomics is a way of thinking, not a set of facts to memorize. This course teaches that way of thinking from scratch — using real examples, real data, and policy cases that connect the material to decisions that actually get made.

The goal is not to turn you into an economist. It is to give you a set of analytical tools — supply and demand, incentive logic, market failures — that make the world more legible. Once you have them, you find them useful in places you did not expect: traffic patterns, environmental policy, healthcare markets, your own choices about time and money.

No prior economics background is assumed or expected. The course is designed for students encountering these ideas for the first time — and it takes that seriously, both in how the material is introduced and in the expectation that engaging with it will require genuine intellectual work.

An introduction to microeconomic analysis. Topics include supply and demand, elasticity, consumer and producer theory, market structures, externalities and public goods, and the role of prices in coordinating economic activity. Policy applications appear throughout: taxes, subsidies, price controls, environmental regulation, and labor market policy. Students complete the course with a working understanding of how economists think — including when markets work well and when they fall short. Active learning methods are used throughout, including classroom simulations, empirical exercises, and policy case discussions. No prior economics background is assumed.

The Lens

What you'll see differently

These are not trick questions. They are the kinds of questions where having an economic framework changes what you notice — and what kind of answer you find convincing. By the end of the term, each of these will look different to you than it does now.

Traffic & Congestion

Why is rush hour congested even though driving is "free"? What would happen to traffic if we charged for road use — and who would that help or hurt?

Rent Control

If a city caps rents to make housing more affordable, what happens to the amount of housing available? Why do economists tend to be skeptical of rent control — and is that skepticism warranted?

Environmental Pollution

If pollution is harmful, why do markets produce so much of it? What tools can fix that — and what are the real tradeoffs between a carbon tax, a cap-and-trade system, and direct regulation?

Healthcare Costs

Why does healthcare cost so much in the United States, and why do so many people lack it? Supply and demand, information asymmetries, and insurance market design each contribute part of the answer.

The Price of Things

Why does water cost more at a stadium than a grocery store? Why do airline prices change by the hour? Why does the same work pay more in cities than in rural areas? Prices contain information — and reading them is a skill.

Your Own Decisions

Sunk costs shouldn't affect your decisions — but they do. You respond to incentives even when you don't notice. Microeconomics gives you a framework for understanding your own choices, which is occasionally unsettling and often useful.

These examples appear throughout the course. Students are encouraged to bring their own examples to class — economics is most interesting when it connects to something you actually care about.

The Toolkit

Three tools that do most of the work

Microeconomics has a large toolkit, but most of the analytical power comes from three core ideas. The course introduces them carefully, applies them repeatedly, and pushes you to use them yourself — not just to recognize them when they appear.

01

Supply & Demand

The foundational model — and a genuine analytical framework, not just two intersecting lines. It tells you how prices and quantities are determined, how they respond to change, and who gains and loses when they do. You will use it constantly, in contexts that don't look like economics until you see how to apply it.

02

Incentives & Behavior

People respond to incentives. Working out exactly how — and in what direction, and with what unintended consequences — is where the analytical work lives. This is the part of the course that most surprises students. The logic is simple; the applications are not.

03

Market Failures & Policy

Markets are powerful. They are also incomplete — failing in predictable ways under predictable conditions. Externalities, public goods, information asymmetries: each produces a characteristic pattern of failure and points toward a characteristic policy response. This is where the course connects most directly to real decisions.

What You'll Do

Thinking, not memorizing

01

Apply the tools

The goal is not to memorize definitions. It is to develop the habit of applying supply and demand, incentive logic, and market failure analysis to situations you encounter — in the news, in policy debates, in your own life. That habit is what sticks after the course ends.

02

Work with real data

Economics is an empirical discipline. We look at real markets and real policy changes — asking what actually happened and whether it matches what the theory predicts. Sometimes it does. Sometimes the data is more interesting than the model.

03

Engage with policy

Every topic in the course has policy implications. You will read about real debates — minimum wages, carbon taxes, rent control, healthcare — and develop reasoned positions based on analysis, not intuition. Disagreement is expected and welcome.

Before You Enroll

Honest expectations

This course is designed to be the entry point to economics at Carleton. It is accessible — but it is not easy. Here is what you should know going in.

Prior Background

None required or expected. The course assumes you have not studied economics before. If you have, you will find the material familiar at first and more challenging than you expect as it develops.

Mathematics

Algebra-level. We use math to think, not to compute. You will work with graphs, equations, and basic calculations — but nothing beyond what you did in high school math.

Class Format

Active learning. Class time is for problems, discussion, and simulations — not just lecture. Engagement is expected, and it is where most of the learning actually happens.

Workload

The ideas build on each other. Keeping up matters more in economics than in courses where topics are more independent. Falling behind is hard to recover from. Staying current is worth the effort.
